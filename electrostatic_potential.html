2 Electric Potential Energy Differences ( Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE)

The potential energy of an object increases when you do a positive amount of work on it. For example, if you lift a mass from the floor, you increase its potential energy. The work done on an object changes its potential energy, and this change is represented by Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE.

Mathematically, the work done by a force F \mathbf{F} F in moving an object from position a a a to position b b b along a line l l l is

W a → b = ∫ a b F ⋅ d l W_{a\rightarrow b}=\int_a^b \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{l} Wa→b​=∫ab​F⋅dl

and this work changes the potential energy of an object according to

W a → b = P E b − P E a ≡ Δ P E W_{a\rightarrow b} = PE_b-PE_a \equiv \Delta PE Wa→b​=PEb​−PEa​≡ΔPE

where the symbol ≡ \equiv ≡ is used to indicate that Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE is defined to be the final potential at b b b minus the initial potential at a a a.

If a force is always perpendicular to the direction of movement, the work due to that force is zero.

When the force on an object does not change when it is moved a distance L L L from a a a to b b b along a straight line, and the force vector is aligned with the path of movement, then

W a → b = ∫ a b F ⋅ d l = ( ± ) ∣ F ∣ L W_{a\rightarrow b}=\int_a^b \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{l}=(\pm)|\mathbf{F}|L Wa→b​=∫ab​F⋅dl=(±)∣F∣L

where L L L is positive; the + + + sign is used for a force that is in the direction of movement and the − - − sign is used for a force that is in the opposite direction of movement. (You should know how to derive this.) For example, if you lift a mass m m m upwards by a distance L L L, the force you exert is in the same direction of movement, so you do a work of m g L mgL mgL. The gravitational force on the mass is in the opposite direction of movement, so the work done by the gravitational force is − m g L -mgL −mgL.

One of the most common difficulties in calculating work and energy is getting the correct sign for the answer. The following two problems have questions that ensure that you are able to predict the correct sign of work and changes in potential energy.

3 Electric potential difference Δ V \Delta V ΔV

In the previous section, we considered moving an arbitrary amount of charge (either positive or negative) from point a a a to point b b b and computed its change in potential energy Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE.

An electric potential difference Δ V \Delta V ΔV is defined to be the change in electric potential energy of a (positive by convention) test charge when it is moved from point a a a to point b b b divided by the magnitude of the test charge.

As a result, the only difference between the Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE calculations performed previously and Δ V \Delta V ΔV calculations is that we first compute Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE for a + 1 +1 +1 C charge. To get Δ V \Delta V ΔV, we simply divide by Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE by + 1 +1 +1 C.

The definition of electric potential is similar to the definition of electric field in that they both involve the consideration of a test charge. That is, the electric field is the force on a test charge divided by the magnitude of the test charge:

E = F q o \mathbf{E} = \frac{\mathbf{F}}{q_o} E=qo​F​

A change in potential is the change in potential of a test charge divided by the magnitude of the test charge’s charge:

Δ V = Δ P E q o \Delta V = \frac{\Delta PE}{q_o} ΔV=qo​ΔPE​

The advantage of using changes in electric potential, Δ V \Delta V ΔV, as opposed to changes in electric potential energy, Δ P E \Delta PE ΔPE, of a specific amount of charge is that once the electric potential difference Δ V \Delta V ΔV is known for a test charge, the change in potential energy for an arbitrary amount of charge Q Q Q can be computed by simply multiplying Δ V \Delta V ΔV and Q Q Q. This is similar to the advantage of the electric field. If we know the electric field at a given point, we can find the force on an arbitrary charge Q Q Q by multiplying E \mathbf{E} E and Q Q Q.
