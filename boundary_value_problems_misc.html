This problem is similar to Example 3.4, with the difference being the location of the origin.

  1. Find an equation that satisfies two of the four boundary conditions and has two unknown constants, one of them being m m m.

  2. What is the constraint on the values of m m m for this problem?

{| class=”wikitable collapsible collapsed” ! align=”left” | Answer |- | ( A e m x + B e − m x ) sin ⁡ , m y (Ae^{mx} + Be^{-mx})\sin,my (Aemx+Be−mx)sin,my

satisfies the last two boundary conditions provided that m = n π / h m=n\pi/h m=nπ/h, where n n n is a positive integer.

As a prelude to future problems, note that you can relate A A A and B B B by using the first two boundary conditions

V ( x = 0 , y ) = V o = ( A e m 0 + B e − m 0 ) sin ⁡ , m y = ( A + B ) sin ⁡ , m y V(x=0,y) = V_o = (Ae^{m0} + Be^{-m0})\sin,my = (A + B)\sin,my V(x=0,y)=Vo​=(Aem0+Be−m0)sin,my=(A+B)sin,my

and

V ( x = w , y ) = V o = ( A e m w + B e − m w ) sin ⁡ , m y V(x=w,y) = V_o = (Ae^{mw} + Be^{-mw})\sin,my V(x=w,y)=Vo​=(Aemw+Be−mw)sin,my

Combining these two equations gives A = B ( e − m w − 1 ) / ( 1 − e m w ) A=B(e^{-mw}-1)/(1-e^{mw}) A=B(e−mw−1)/(1−emw)

so that

V ( x , y ) = ( A e m x + B e − m x ) ( sin ⁡ , m y ) = B ( e m x ( e − m w − 1 ) / ( 1 − e m w ) + e − m x ) sin ⁡ , m y V(x,y) = (Ae^{mx} + Be^{-mx})(\sin,my) = B(e^{mx}(e^{-mw}-1)/(1-e^{mw}) + e^{-mx})\sin,my V(x,y)=(Aemx+Be−mx)(sin,my)=B(emx(e−mw−1)/(1−emw)+e−mx)sin,my

Although we have only satisfied two of the four boundary conditions, the equation

(A) V ( x , y ) = B ( e m x ( e − m w − 1 ) / ( 1 − e m w ) + e − m x ) sin ⁡ , m y V(x,y) = B(e^{mx}(e^{-mw}-1)/(1-e^{mw}) + e^{-mx})\sin,my V(x,y)=B(emx(e−mw−1)/(1−emw)+e−mx)sin,my

gives the same equation for the first two boundaries at x = 0 x=0 x=0 and x = w x=w x=w of

V ( x = 0 , y ) = V ( x = w , y ) = B [ ( e − m w − e m w ) / ( 1 − e m w ) ] sin ⁡ , m y V(x=0,y) = V(x=w,y) = B\left[(e^{-mw}-e^{mw})/(1-e^{mw})\right]\sin,my V(x=0,y)=V(x=w,y)=B[(e−mw−emw)/(1−emw)]sin,my,

which can be shown by subtitution of x = 0 x=0 x=0 and x = w x=w x=w into (A) and simplifying.

When doing Fourier’s trick, x x x is replaced with its boundary value. So doing the Fourier Trick for the first boundary condition at x = 0 x=0 x=0 using (A) would start with

V ( x = 0 , y ) = V o = B 1 [ ( e − m w − e m w ) / ( 1 − e m w ) ] sin ⁡ , m y + B 2 [ ( e − m w − e m w ) / ( 1 − e m w ) ] sin ⁡ , m y + … V(x=0,y) = V_o = B_1\left[(e^{-mw}-e^{mw})/(1-e^{mw})\right]\sin,my + B_2\left[(e^{-mw}-e^{mw})/(1-e^{mw})\right]\sin,my +… V(x=0,y)=Vo​=B1​[(e−mw−emw)/(1−emw)]sin,my+B2​[(e−mw−emw)/(1−emw)]sin,my+…

which is the same as the equation one would start with for the second boundary condition at x = w x=w x=w using (A):

V ( x = w , y ) = V o = B 1 [ ( e − m w − e m w ) / ( 1 − e m w ) ] sin ⁡ , m y + B 2 [ ( e − m w − e m w ) / ( 1 − e m w ) ] sin ⁡ , m y + … V(x=w,y) = V_o = B_1\left[(e^{-mw}-e^{mw})/(1-e^{mw})\right]\sin,my + B_2\left[(e^{-mw}-e^{mw})/(1-e^{mw})\right]\sin,my+… V(x=w,y)=Vo​=B1​[(e−mw−emw)/(1−emw)]sin,my+B2​[(e−mw−emw)/(1−emw)]sin,my+…

Therefore, even though only the second two boundaries have been matched, the Fourier analysis needed to make the first two match will be identical. So Fourier’s trick only needs to be applied once.
