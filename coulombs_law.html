Notation

  • A primed vector, (\mathbf{r}’) is usually used for location of a charge that is exerting a force on another charge. An unprimed vector (\mathbf{r}) is usually used for the location of the charge for which the force is being calculated.

== Derivation ==

Suppose that Newton uniformly charged a spherical shell and found that everywhere inside the shell, a small test charge experienced no force. When the test charge was placed outside of the shell, it moved radially away from the shell. Provide an explanation for why it is reasonable for him to conclude that the force between two charges will depend on the inverse of the square of their separation and be directed along a straight line that connects them.

{| class=”wikitable collapsible collapsed” ! align=”left” | Answer |- | This is somewhat of a subtle question written to emphasize some of the important properties of an inverse square force law. Newton’s Law of gravity, which has a magnitude of (F=GmM/d^2) and direction along the straight line that connects the masses, is an inverse square law. In Physics I, you learned that particle inside of an empty shell felt zero force everywhere inside of the shell. Outside of the shell, the mass experienced a radial force.
