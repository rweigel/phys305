1 Introduction

For a single point charge q q q at r ′ \mathbf{r}' r′, the potential at any location in space is

V ( r ) = k q ԉ = q ∣ r − r ′ ∣ V(\mathbf{r})=k{\frac{q}{\char"0509}}=\frac{q}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|} V(r)=kԉq​=∣r−r′∣q​

Often we are interested in an approximation of this potential at points that are far away.

Previously, the binomial expansion was used to find the electric field for point charges along an axis and only at locations on the same axis. The same approximation procedure that is used for the electric field may be used for potential. (In general, it is often easier to start with finding the scalar V V V and then using it to find the vector E \mathbf{E} E.)

In the following, a general equation for potential at any location in space due to a point charge at any location in space is developed by considering two generalizations:

  1. finding the potential at any location in space due to a point charge on the z z z–axis and then

  2. finding the potential at any location in space due to a point charge at any location in space.

See also Chapter 3.4 of Griffiths.

3 Generalization 2

When both the charge and point of interest are at arbitrary locations in space, the angle between r \mathbf{r} r and r ′ \mathbf{r}' r′ is no longer the spherical polar angle, θ \theta θ. In this case, the angle α \alpha α between them will depend on both θ \theta θ and ϕ \phi ϕ. From the definition of the dot product, cos ⁡ α \cos\alpha cosα is given by

cos ⁡ α = r ⋅ r ′ ∣ r ∣ ∣ r ′ ∣ = r ⋅ r ′ r r ′ \cos\alpha = \frac{\mathbf{r}\boldsymbol{\cdot}\mathbf{r}'}{|\mathbf{r}||\mathbf{r}'|}=\frac{\mathbf{r}\boldsymbol{\cdot}\mathbf{r}'}{rr'} cosα=∣r∣∣r′∣r⋅r′​=rr′r⋅r′​

To derive an expansion for this configuration, the same steps used in Generalization I can be used but with the replacement of z ′ z' z′ with r ′ r' r′ and θ \theta θ with α \alpha α. With this replacement, we have

V = k q r [ 1 + r ′ r cos ⁡ α + ( r ′ r ) 2 ( 3 cos ⁡ 2 α − 1 2 ) + … ] V=\frac{kq}{r}\left[1+\frac{r'}{r}\cos\alpha + \left(\frac{r'}{r}\right)^2\left(\frac{3\cos^2\alpha-1}{2}\right)+ …\right] V=rkq​[1+rr′​cosα+(rr′​)2(23cos2α−1​)+…]
