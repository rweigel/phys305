1 Introduction

The electric potential due to a “pure” electric dipole centered on the origin is

V(r)=4πϵo​1​r2p⋅r^​

and the electric field is

E(r)=4πϵo​1​r31​(3(p⋅r^)r^−p)

(Griffiths defines a “pure” dipole on page 159; when r/d→∞, where d is the separation distance between the charges, the field of the dipole approaches being purely due to the 1/r2 term in the expansion of the potential due to the two charges.)

If the dipole is not at the origin, then we need to replace r with ԉ and r^ with ԉ^. Then the electric potential and field at r due to an electric dipole with its center at r′ is

V(r)=4πϵo​1​ԉ2p⋅ԉ^​

and the electric field is

E(r)=4πϵo​1​ԉ31​(3(p⋅ԉ^)ԉ^−p)

If the dipoles can be considered as being continuously distributed, then the differential potential, dV, due to a differential dipole, dp is

dV(r)=4πϵo​1​ԉ2dp⋅ԉ^​

If we define an electric dipole density P according to

dp(r′)=P(r′)dτ′

then

dV(r)=4πϵo​1​ԉ2P⋅ԉ^​dτ′

and the total potential at a point r is

V(r)=4πϵo​1​∫ԉ2P⋅ԉ^​dτ′

A corresponding equation for E could also be derived. However, it is usually easier to compute E by first computing V.

1.1 Example – Computation of V using P

  1. Compute the potential on the z–axis using V=4πϵo​1​∫ԉ2P⋅ԉ^​dτ′

  2. Show that the result for V(z) is idential to the equation for potential for two disks centerd on z=±h/2 with σ=±Po​.

Answer

1.

r′=s′s^′+z′z^=x′cosϕ′x^+y′sinϕ′y^​+z′z^

Using

dz′d​(s′2+(z−z′)2)−1/2=(s′2+(z−z′)2)3/2z−z′​

Gives

V(z)=4πϵo​1​2πPo​∫0R​s′ds′∫−h/2h/2​dzd​(s′2+(z−z′)2)−1/2dz′

V(z)=4πϵo​1​2πPo​∫0R​s′ds′[(s′2+(z−z′)2)−1/21​]−h/2h/2​

and

V(z)=4πϵo​1​2πPo​∫0R​s′ds′[(s′2+(z−h/2)2)−1/21​−(s′2+(z+h/2)2)−1/21​]

  1. These two integrals are the same as the integrals one would obtain for computing V(z) for the two disks. In this case, the starting equation would be V(z)=∫σda′/ԉ. 2 Alternative form of V

The equation

V(r)=4πϵo​1​∫ԉ2P⋅ԉ^​dτ′

can be written as

V(r)=4πϵo​1​(∫V​ԉρb​​dτ′+∮ԉσb​​da′)

where the closed integral is taken over the closed surface of volume V and

σb​≡P⋅n^ (with P evaluated on the surface.)

and

ρb​≡−∇⋅P.

A partial proof is given in the Appendix.

Recall that for regular (non–bound) charges on a surface or in a volume, the equation for V is

V(r)=4πϵo​1​(∫V​ԉρ​dτ′+∮ԉσ​da′)

This has the same form as the new equation

V(r)=4πϵo​1​(∫V​ԉρb​​dτ′+∮ԉσb​​da′)

The implication is that we can use the same techniques used to find V or E created by charge densities σ and ρ for problems with σb​ and ρb​. If we encounter a problem where ρb​ and σb​ (1) are on the same surfaces and (2) have the same spatial dependence as ρ and σ in a problem solved previously, we can use the previous solution with the replacement of ρ with ρb​ and σ with σb​.

2.1 Example

A solid cylinder of radius R and height h is aligned with the z-axis, centered on the origin, and has a polarization P=Po​z^.

  1. Compute σb​ and ρb​.

  2. Compute E(z)

Answer

This problem was solved earlier using the formula

V(r)=4πϵo​1​∫ԉ2P⋅ԉ^​dτ′

Here it is solved using the alternative form for V that involves bound charge densities.

ρb​=−∇⋅P=0 can be shown by direct calculation using the divergence formula in either cartesian or cylindrical coordinates. Using σb​≡P⋅n^, on the top/bottom caps n^=±z^ so σb​=±Po​. On the side surface, s^, so σb​=0.

To find the electric field, we could compute the potential using

V(r)=4πϵo​1​∮ԉσb​​da′

and then compute E=−∇V. Or, we can simply use the equation for the electric field on the z–axis due to a uniformly charged disk found previously:

E=2ϵ0​σz​(∣z∣1​−R2+z2​1​)z^

This equation is for a uniformly charged disk in the x–y plane and centered on the origin. The field due to σb​ on the top surface is this equation with the replacement of σ with Po​ and z with z−h/2. The field due to σb​ on the bottom surface is this equation with the replacement of σ with −Po​ and z with z+h/2. The total field is the sum of these two fields.

3 Interpretation of Bound Surface Charges

In 4.2 of Griffiths, he models a polarized sphere by using two uniformly charged spheres with centers that are separated by a small distance d. One sphere has a postive charge and the other a negative charge. He then computes the electric field in the overlap region and in the region outside of both spheres where there is no overlap.

In this problem, a polarized slab will be modeled using two slabs of charge with uniform and opposite charge density that are offset by a small distance d.

  1. Find E(y) for the slab with uniform charge density ρo​ shown in the following figure. Assume that the slab is infinite in extent in the ±z and ±x directions so that Gauss’s law can be used. (This slab can be thought of as being composed of thin sheets of charge stacked together and so an alternative to using Gauss’s law is to sum the electric field due to sheets of charge.)

  2. Plot E(y) vs y.

  3. Next, compute and plot E(y) for the same slab if it had charge density of −ρo​ and was shifted by −d in the y–direction. Assume that d≪t.

  4. Compute E in the region of overlap of the +ρo​ and −ρo​ slabs.

Answer

Details on how to solve this problem were given in class and so only a summary is given here.

1. Gauss’s law can be used to find the field for ∣y∣≥t/2 (a cylinder centered on the origin or with its bottom cap at y=0 can both be used). This gives Ey​=±ρo​t/2ϵo​ above/below the slab. Inside the slab, we know Ey​=0 at y=0 because the field due to the upper part of the slab cancels that due to the lower part. We also expect that inside the slab, Ey​(y) field will increase linearly (why?). From this, we can write Ey​(y)=ρo​y/ϵo​. This equation gives zero at the origin and matches the outer field at y=±t/2. Alternatively, we can also use Gauss’s law. For a cylinder centered on the origin and height 2y, the charge enclosed is ρo​2y.

2.

3. Invert the plot from 2. and then translate it by d in the y direction. Inside the negatively charged slab, the field will be Ey​(y)=−ρo​(y+d)/ϵo​. (This gives Ey​=0 when y=−d, corresponding to the center of the negatively charged slab.)

4. In the region of overlap we need to sum

Ey​(y)=Ey+​(y)+Ey−​(y)

Using Ey+​(y)=ρo​y/ϵo​ and Ey−​(y)=−ρo​(y+d)/ϵo​ gives

Ey​(y)=−ρo​d/ϵo​

This is the same field one would get if we had a positive sheet of charge at y=t/2 and a negative sheet at y=−t/2 if the sheets had a charge density of ρo​d. If one draws the charge density when the two slabs are superimposed, the system appears to be two such sheets of charge.

4.1 Thick infinite slab

P=Po​z^ in the region ∣z∣<t on a slab that is infinite in the x and y directions.

  1. Find σb​ and ρb​

  2. Find Eb​

Answer

On the top surface, σb​=+Po​ (because n^=z^) and on the bottom surface, σb​=−Po​ (because n^=−z^). The bound volume charge density is ρb​=−∇⋅P=0.

To find E due to this polarized slab, we need to find E created by two infinite sheets of charge that are parallel with to the x–y plane. The sheets have σ=±σb​ and are at z=±t/2. This problem has been considered before and the result is E=−σ/ϵo​z^ between the sheets and zero outside. Therefore, E=−Po​/ϵo​z^ betwen the sheets and E=0 outside. See also HW #8 where a polarized slab was created by superimposing two slabs of charge density ±ρo​ and then shifting one down by a small distance.

  • The net polarization charge is zero. Is this expected?

  • What is the field if two of these slabs are stacked together to form a slab of thickness 2t?

% Copied to notes

A sphere of radius Ro​ has a spherical cavity of radius Ri​. The sphere and cavity are centered on the origin. The region Ri​≤r≤Ro​ has a polarization P=(Po​r2/Ri2​)r^.

  1. Find σb​ and ρb​.

  2. Find Eb​(r), which is the electric field due to the bound charge densities found in part 1. of this problem.

Answer

  1. σb​=−Po​ at r=Ri​ and σb​=Po​Ro2​/Ri2​ at r=Ro​. ρb​=−4Po​r/Ri2​.

    A common error is to write σb​ in terms of r. Remember that the equation for σb​ is evaluated on the surface. In this problem, the surface is at either r=Ri​ or r=Ro​.

  2. One can use Gauss’s law with the above charge densities to find: E=0 for r<Ri​ and r>Ro​ and E=−Pr​(r)r^/ϵo​, where Pr​(r)=Po​r2/Ri2​.

    It is easier to use Gauss’s law for dielectrics. Inside the sphere, there is no free charge, so ∮D⋅dA=0. From symmetry arguments (what are they?), we can write D=Dr​(r)r^ and so the integral reduces to Dr​4πr2=0⇒Dr​=0 for all r>0. Using the definition of D=ϵo​E+P with P=0 for 0<r<Ri​ and r>Ro​ gives E=0 in these regions. Er​=0 at r=0 from a symmetry argument (what is it?). Inside the sphere, P is non–zero and Dr​=0⇒Er​+Pr​/ϵ0​⇒Er​=−Pr​/ϵo​ as before.

n^=r^, so σb​=P⋅n^=Po​z^⋅r^

5 Appendix – Partial Proof of Alternative form of V

Consider a point far enough away that dipole term dominates.

Assume a dipole volume density of

P(x′)=Δv′⟨p(x′)⟩​

where ⟨p(x′)⟩ is the average dipole moment in the small volume Δv′

For a single “perfect” (or “pure” or “ideal”) dipole at the origin

ΦpO​​(x)=4πϵo​1​r3p⋅x​

For a “perfect” dipole at x′, make the replacements r≡∣x∣→∣x−x′∣ and x→x−x′

Φp′​(x)=4πϵo​1​∣x−x′∣3p(x′)⋅(x−x′)​

Using P(x′)=⟨p(x′)⟩/Δv′, the potential at x due to the differential volume Δv′

ΦΔv′​(x)=4πϵo​1​∣x−x′∣3P(x′)⋅(x−x′)​Δv′

where x′ now means the location of the differential volume Δv′. For Δv′→0, Δv′→d3x′; summing over all Δv′ in V gives

Φ(x)=4πϵo​1​∫V​∣x−x′∣3P(x′)⋅(x−x′)​d3x′

Using

∇′(∣x−x′∣1​)=∣x−x′∣3x−x′​

this can be written as

Φ(x)=4πϵo​1​∫V​P(x′)⋅∇′(∣x−x′∣1​)d3x′

It is important to remember that we assumed x was outside of the volume of integration V and that the dipoles are perfect. It can be shown that this same equation applies to points inside the volume and when the dipoles are not perfect (See Zangwell or Griffiths; Jackson also addresses in Chapter 4.1, but does not mention in derivation similar to the above that appears in Chapter 4.3).

Integration by parts (or the identity ∇⋅(fF)=F⋅∇f+f∇⋅F)

Φ(x)=∫V​∇′⋅(∣x−x′∣P(x′)​)d3x′+∫V​∣x−x′∣−∇′⋅P(x′)​d3x′

The first term can be rewritten using the divergence theorem

Φ(x)=∮S​∣x−x′∣P(x′)⋅n^,​da′+∫V​∣x−x′∣−∇′⋅P(x′)​d3x′

Compare this equation with the equation for the potential due to a charge density ρ and surface charge density σ

Φ(x)=∮S​∣x−x′∣σ(x′)​da′+∫V​∣x−x′∣ρ(x′)​d3x′

from which it followed that

∇⋅E=ϵo​ρ​

If we define “bound” densities σb​≡P⋅n^ and ρb​≡−∇⋅P

Φb​(x)=∮S​∣x−x′∣σb​(x′)​da+∫V​∣x−x′∣ρb​(x′)​d3x′

In addition, one can compute the electric field, Eb​, due to the bound charge density using Eb​=∇Φ.

At this point, we have essentially considered a system of two types of charges: single charges, represented by ρf​ and pure dipoles, represented by ρb​. There is a class of problems one can solve where you are given a fixed (or “frozen-in”) P and are asked to find Φ. In this case, one simply computes σb​ and ρb​ and integrates to find Φ. Quite often, one does not even need to do an integration - if we know the potential for an ordinary charge distribution σ (or ρ) and we find σb​ (or ρ) has the same distribution, we only need to replace σ (or ρ) with the value of σb​ (or ρb​) in terms of P.

See the description for [[#Magnetized_Objects]] for a way of thinking about how an object gets polarized. The main difference is that an electric field causes the random dipoles to become aligned anti-parallel to an external electric field. If the object is somehow “frozen” so the non-random dipoles can’t randomize after the external field is turned off, you get a polarized object. Most materials are such that the induced polarization electric field tends to oppose the external electric field (dielectrics). In contrast to magnetizable objects which can enhance (paramagnets) or oppose (diamagnets) the external magnetic field.

The mnemonic I mention in class for the prefix is that “di” or “dia” means the object wants to “die” (kill) the external field. (From a language perspective, dielectrics should be called diaelectrics since dia in Greek is “oppose”, but Faraday decided it did not look right and so used “di”. If you happened to know the prefix “di” means double in Greek, you would be a bit upset about this decision; perhaps he should have used “un-di-electrics” …).
