1 Introduction

In Continuous Electric Dipole Distributions, the problem of finding the electric field due to an object that had a polarization P \mathbf{P} P “frozen in” was considered.

The approach was to find the bound charge densities that result from this polarization. Then the electric field due to the bound charge densities was computed.

Consider an object that is unpolarized ( P = 0 \mathbf{P}=0 P=0), but is placed in an external electric field, E e x t \mathbf{E}_{ext} Eext​. In response to this electric field, atomic–sized dipoles will tend to form. Thes induced dipoles create an electric field, which called an induced electric field.

The total field is then

E t o t a l = E e x t e r n a l + E i n d u c e d \mathbf{E}_{total}=\mathbf{E}_{external} + \mathbf{E}_{induced} Etotal​=Eexternal​+Einduced​

By convention, we drop the “total” subscript and abbreviate “external” as “ext”. Given that the induced field is can be computed from bound charge densities, we can use E b \mathbf{E}_b Eb​ in place of E i n d u c e d \mathbf{E}_{induced} Einduced​ to emphasize this point. In this notation, we have

E = E e x t + E b \mathbf{E}=\mathbf{E}_{ext} + \mathbf{E}_{b} E=Eext​+Eb​

It is important to note that E b \mathbf{E}_b Eb​ depends on E \mathbf{E} E.

Without additional information, it is not possible to compute E \mathbf{E} E – we need to know how much P \mathbf{P} P is generated given E \mathbf{E} E. Once we have P \mathbf{P} P, we can compute the bound charge densities and then E b \mathbf{E}_b Eb​.

For many materials, the relationship between P \mathbf{P} P and E \mathbf{E} E is linear:

P = ϵ o χ e E \mathbf{P}=\epsilon_o\chi_e\mathbf{E} P=ϵo​χe​E

where the constant χ e \chi_e χe​ is the electric susceptibility.

A constant that often appears in induced polarization problems is ϵ o ( 1 + χ e ) \epsilon_o(1+\chi_e) ϵo​(1+χe​), which is called permittivity or the dielectric constant:

ϵ ≡ ϵ o ( 1 + χ e ) \epsilon\equiv \epsilon_o(1+\chi_e) ϵ≡ϵo​(1+χe​)
